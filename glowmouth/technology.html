The Technology

How GlowMouth works at a technical level.

Two components work together — a hardware imaging system and an AI analysis layer. Here is what each does, how it was built, and where its limits are.

Hardware

High-Resolution Intraoral Imaging

GlowMouth uses Quantitative Light-Induced Fluorescence (QLF) — a peer-reviewed imaging method developed and validated in dental research since the 1990s. QLF has been studied across dozens of published trials for its ability to detect early-stage enamel demineralization, plaque distribution, and bacterial film on tooth surfaces.

The device emits light at 405nm (violet-blue spectrum). Healthy tooth enamel fluoresces predictably at this wavelength. Areas with plaque, bacterial film, or early demineralization absorb rather than reflect that fluorescence — creating measurable contrast that the camera captures in each frame.

Five standard capture angles are defined by the app's guided overlay: front, left side, right side, upper arch, lower arch. Consistency across sessions is essential for valid trend tracking.

Imaging method Quantitative Light-Induced Fluorescence (QLF)
Wavelength 405nm (violet-blue spectrum)
Field of view 5 standard intraoral angles per session
Capture time Under 2 minutes
Resolution High-res CMOS sensor; blur detection filters low-quality frames
Limitation

GlowMouth is not a diagnostic device. It does not perform radiographic imaging and cannot detect subsurface decay, bone loss, periodontal pocket depth, or any condition requiring X-ray or clinical probing. QLF imaging is a surface-level optical method. Its outputs are directional wellness signals, not clinical diagnoses.

Analysis

Clinically-Informed AI Models

The GlowMouth AI layer uses a convolutional neural network (CNN) trained on labeled intraoral images to detect surface patterns in your scan. Each frame is analyzed independently. The model outputs a probability score for each indicator category based on patterns it has learned to associate with plaque-like film, gum tissue changes, and enamel surface characteristics.

Per-frame scores are aggregated across all five angles to produce indicator-level readings and the overall GlowScore. Aggregating across multiple angles reduces the influence of any single low-quality or ambiguous frame.

The model was trained with input from practicing dentists who reviewed and labeled images for plaque distribution, gum status, and enamel condition. Clinical advisors compared model outputs against their own assessments during development to validate directional accuracy.

Model type CNN (convolutional neural network)
Training data Clinician-labeled intraoral QLF datasets
Output categories 3 tissue types: plaque film, gum tissue, enamel
Update cadence Cloud-deployed; on-device inference for latency
Limitation

The model has not been formally validated as a Class II medical device. Its outputs are directional wellness indicators, not clinical measurements. Image quality directly affects output reliability — a Low confidence result should be treated as inconclusive. We do not claim a specific accuracy percentage because that claim would require the formal clinical validation we have not yet completed.

Regulatory Approach

A transparent account of where we stand.

GlowMouth is designed with a clear regulatory roadmap. Here is what applies today and what we are building toward.

Year 1–2 — Current

General Wellness

At launch, GlowMouth operates under FDA General Wellness Policy guidance. It is a wellness monitoring tool, not a cleared diagnostic device. Its outputs are not intended to substitute for professional dental diagnosis or treatment.

Products under General Wellness Policy scope promote general health awareness and are not associated with specific disease management. GlowMouth's GlowScore and indicator readings fit this definition — they provide awareness and habit feedback, not clinical assessment.

No 510(k) required at launch under this scope.

Year 3 Target

Class II SaMD

GlowMouth is on a pathway toward FDA 510(k) clearance as a Class II Software as a Medical Device (SaMD). This requires clinical evidence of substantial equivalence to a predicate device, independent validation studies, and formal Quality Management System compliance.

We are building toward this milestone in parallel with our clinical advisor partnerships and VCU research collaborations. This path is ongoing — it has not been completed. GlowMouth should not be treated as a cleared SaMD until clearance is obtained and publicly disclosed.
